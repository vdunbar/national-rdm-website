Ownership, Copyright, Licensing, Control, Access, and Reuse

The collection, storage, use, and dissemination of research data as they relate to ownership, copyright, licencing, control, access, and reuse are influenced and governed by many frameworks within multiple jurisdictions. Many jurisdictions stand in contradiction or in conflict with each other. It is a complex landscape. This chapter explores some of these issues, points to sources of information or tools to facilitate navigating research data ownership, copyright, licencing, access, and reuse. 

In a Western legal framework, the dominant structure governing academia, data have been tied to both concepts of common knowledge as well as intellectual property. In their original form, many data are deemed not to be owned; for example, environmental data like temperatures, or counting observations, like the number of pedestrians crossing a street. This suggests that simply collecting data does not bring with it intrinsic notions of ownership or control of that data. Providing some intellectual enhancement to those data, however, may protect that specific dataset through legal structures designed to protect intellectual property rights; say that same temperature or count data is cleaned, organized and packaged to enable specific types of analyses; this package, within this framework, may be protected by intellectual property rights. 

Within western academic practices, collecting data - whether by interview, body sensors, video footage, etc. - and determining ownership, copyright, licencing, control, access, and reuse of these data is governed by a whole series of other frameworks, some rooted in western legal tradition and governed by, for example, institutions (policies, standards, and guidelines to which employees and students must adhere), funder mandates (legally binding funding agreements), and partnerships (research agreements, which may be documents of good faith or legally binding), while others are codified in systems of practice that align with cultural norms through entities like research ethics boards or disciplinary codes of ethics. These frameworks of research data governance and ownership can cross jurisdictional boundaries; research conducted across more than one institution may have misaligned policies, standards, and guidelines and research conducted in more than one country may have to reconcile different legal requirements. 

Not all data are strictly governed by western legal traditions and frameworks. More recently, academic institutions, particularly in a colonial context, are recognizing that they must work within and alongside frameworks that reside outside these traditions. In a colonial context this means recognizing Indigenous sovereignty and respecting Indigenous Peoples’ data; this is discussed in more detail elsewhere in this text. 

When working across multiple frameworks and jurisdictions, researchers may encounter conflicts and contradictions and may struggle to reconcile traditions, practices, and frameworks that simply do not translate and that exist in contexts of deep-seated distrust. 

Note 

Not explicitly articulated above, but integral to much of the discussion, relates to your data source. Your data source will likely bring with it limitations on what you can do with that data whether those limitations be rooted in legal systems, relationships, or responsible and ethical approaches to the conduct of research. We often articulate this in relation to people we work with, but gathering data from texts or stories that are otherwise protected will impose similar limits. 

Ownership

Ownership in the context of research data is a messy topic. We start with ownership here since copyright and licencing are predicated on concepts of ownership. Yet, while it is true that copyright and licencing are predicated on ownership, this is restricted to specific legal frameworks that assign ownership to individuals or individual entities, which is quite distinct from those frameworks that recognize collective ownership and responsibility. 

In the context of a graduate research lab, an important question you should ask that relates to ownership is whether you are allowed to take the data that underpins your research with you when you leave, whether that is pursuing your career in your own lab at the same institution or moving to a new institution. For this reason, it is important to discuss with your supervisor how ownership of data is attributed. If your research involves working with any partners, whether that be industry, government, non-profit, ad-hoc etc, ownership of the data should be a very early discussion. Ultimately, ownership will impact virtually every aspect of control that you have over the research data - where it is stored, how it is licenced, how it can be shared, etc. Documentation should always be developed to attest to whatever agreements on data ownership are put in place. 

In addition to the above, aspects of ownership may be governed by funding agreements, research agreements, and institutional policies. For agreements, be sure you are familiar with and comfortable with whatever is stipulated in these. It is likely that as a graduate student you will not have direct access to your Office of Research Services, which is the primary portal through which support for these would be provided. Your supervisor or project primary investigator (PI) will by default then be your primary support here. Institutional policies about data ownership will be distributed through your Chief Information Officer (CIO), and you should be able to find relevant policies, standards, and guidelines through them. Interpretation of these documents is often another matter and may require consultation with peers, mentors, and members of your research support units. 

Copyright

Copyright is a specific legal construct found in western legal practices and centered on a notion of the ownership of intellectual property. It is important to note that copyright, ownership, and licences are intertwined. This section is not about copyright per se, but there are some relevant aspects of copyright to consider in the context of research data and RDM. 

In an academic context, interpretation of copyright law will be handled by a dedicated legal team, and dissemination of this interpretation is often handled through the institution’s library. 

Licensing

A data license, attached to a dataset governed by copyright, defines what external parties may do with a dataset. A licence is a standard use protocol; without a licence, anyone wishing to use a copyrighted object would need to explicitly contact the copyright holder and negotiate a term of use. Licencing data impacts the ease with which data may be reused. A standard, well documented licence clearly and accessibly communicates how research data may be used. Bespoke licences require unique attention. The lack of a licence requires personal communication. 

It is important to note that some funding and publication agreements require data sharing, and this requirement may include application of a specific licence. It is prudent to fully comprehend any agreement and its details related to data sharing in advance of signing off. 

A variety of data license options exist to reflect different requirements and desires, with different institutions, funding bodies, and geographic organizations having their own preferences. It is often more expedient to consult with experts at your institution when considering which license to apply to your datasets. They have the most current information and can walk you through the process and help you avoid pitfalls that you might not be aware exist when selecting licenses. You may also refer to the resources section for a list of common, standard licences.  

Control

Related to, but distinct from data ownership, is the idea of data control, which outlines the individual or body in which research groups have designated to make decisions in regard to how data and research materials are created, collected, and handled throughout the entire data lifecycle. 

While you may be the collector, owner, or licence holder on your data, your control over that data may be limited or partially defined by other factors, such as institutional policies, ethics requirements, funder or publisher mandates, research agreements, etc. Control also comes into play when we consider data collected from or on individuals, in the context of Indigenous Peoples’ data, or other research activities that involve partners. 

Note 

Specifically in the context of Indigenous Peoples’ data, a number of platforms and frameworks have been developed to help address issues and concerns related to control and access to data, and research collaborations continue to develop new approaches to ensure that such data adhere to principles like the CARE principles. Two projects that might be of particular interest to readers to explore this further include Mukurtu, a platform for managing and sharing Indigenous cultural heritage, and the Traditional Knowledge and Biocultural Labels and Notices, developed through Local Contexts, to advance Indigenous data sovereignty and Indigenous innovation. 

Access

Data access is a two-way street. You may need to access someone else’s data, and you need a way to know how, where, and under what restrictions that access looks like. Someone may wish to access your data, and you need to provide a way for others to know how, where, and under what restrictions that access looks like. Access is often independent of reuse, and helps support things like research transparency and reproducibility, so we discuss data reuse separately below. 

You should note that access governance is often embedded in research data platforms that are designed to help support long term storage, data deposit for publication, and data sharing. Generally, your library can help you navigate these platforms and the necessary information required for deposit and defining access. Here, we will look the role of data availability statements that accompany publications and the role of specific positions at an institution or organization in controlling access to research data. 

Data Access Statements 

Data Access Statements, or Data Availability Statements are a section of a scholarly publication that succinctly articulates if and/or how the project’s data can be accessed. In the event that elements of a publication’s data are restricted, they also provide an explanation for this protection. 

A data access statement should include: 

  • Where data can be accessed, such as a specific repository 

  • A persistent access point or numbers, such as a DOI or URL, for the employed data 

  • Any restrictions on enclosed data 

  • Reasons for data restrictions 

Accessing Others’ Data 

It is crucial to ensure that any third-party data collected or employed in your project is also secured with the correct permissions for its reuse with respect for copyright. You should give serious consideration to whether it will be important (or even integral) to be able to share the third-party data. Confirmation of received permission should be documented and preserved in this event. Practicing and documenting respectful access practices towards others’ data will enhance the legitimacy of your own efforts. 

Data Access is often controlled by Data Stewards, who should be the first point of contact in initiating a data access request. Universities will often have specific forms to be completed, but some organizations may rely on direct communication. 

Any requests for others’ data (and requests for your own) should include: 

  • Identification information, such as the name of the administrative entity (faculty) applying 

  • The reason for your request and an explanation of how their data would be used 

  • An explanation of your own data security controls and request an understanding of the provider’s requirements 

  • Duration of proposed access 

Depending on the data, you may also be required to secure ethics board support, personal permission from contributors, or perform a privacy impact assessment. 

Reuse

When using previously collected data, it is critical to understand the genesis of those data: who has ownership over them, who has control over their distribution, what aspects of copyright or other legal or parallel frameworks pertain to them, how they are licenced, and what level of control you will have over those data going forward. In thinking about RDM and documentation, as a researcher, it is your responsibility to equally echo all of these elements for your own research data.
