File Permissions and Ownership

Note 

This section addresses assigning and understanding user roles as they relate to files that are stored digitally. For a discussion on permissions and ownership in a legal or social framework, see the sections on licencing and partnership research. 

Maintaining access permissions to digital files is a critical component of any project for a variety of reasons. Datasets, especially those with sensitive or personal information, have the potential to be exposed to unauthorized people, causing a variety of negative consequences. Furthermore, access permissions and ownership enable an explanation of where data came from and who modified it, allowing user errors to be detected and corrected. 

The granting of access and permissions can exist in the storage platforms, software, and physical devices (laptops, hard drives, etc.,) that you are using. More complex projects with sensitive data can require more complex solutions, which may require working with your institutional support offices, or potentially hiring somebody to manage these. 

Most cloud storage platforms allow for easy permission setting at the file and folder levels. 

Access and Permissions Management

Not everyone involved in a given project necessarily needs or should have access to every aspect of that project. 

When preparing for a research project and mapping out the directory structure and storage locations that will be used, you should think about the following roles and which should have access to what parts of a project: 

  • Primary investigator 

  • Lead researcher 

  • Lab manager 

  • Research collaborators 

  • Graduate research assistants 

  • Undergraduate research assistants 

  • Funders 

  • Editors and Reviewers 

  • The public 

Depending on the makeup of your group, some of these actors may not be relevant, and some may need further breakdown when thinking about roles in the research project and access and permission needs. 

Levels of Access

Most systems allow three levels of access: 

  1. Administrator or owner 

  2. Read and write 

  3. Read-only 

Generally, (1) administrators have full control over whatever they preside on, whether at the project, directory, or file level. At the project level, they can generally invite and remove collaborators and make public or delete files. (2) Read and write will allow a user or person to open (read) and edit (write) existing files. Depending on the system, read and write may allow users to delete, create, or make public files and directories. Generally, read and write does not allow invitation or deletion of collaborators. However, you should ask these questions about whatever storage solution you use to house your research. (3) Read-only generally only allows for the opening of a file, without the ability to edit, delete, or invite additional collaborators. 

One additional level of access is anonymous access. It is becoming increasingly common to support activities like the double-blind peer review process. Generally, this access is read only, providing peer reviewers with access to data and other supplementary files, but in a way that hides the identity of those who have created or worked on those files. 

Ownership

Closely related to access is the notion of file and folder ownership. It is common that the owner will be the creator of the file or folder. In some cases, a user can create a file or folder in a location they are not the owner of, and ownership will be granted to the owner of the location (i.e. the folder or project containing the newly created file or folder). 

Levels of Knowledge

It is worth considering two other potential levels associated with file permissions and access: 

  • Knowing something exists 

  • No knowledge 

Although awareness of information does not inherently merit documentation, it can be valuable to control even rudimentary knowledge around sensitive data. An example of knowing something exists would be an undergraduate research assistant knowing there is a project budget but having no access to the documentation. Correspondingly, no knowledge access is exemplified by a project having a need-to-know basis for sensitive data, wherein unconnected project members or collaborators have neither access nor awareness of data outside their work. 

Even when someone cannot read a file’s contents, we may wish to make them aware of its existence. Publishing, or making publicly available, a copy of your directory structure, is one way of doing this; you may be able to see the names of individual files and perhaps a readme file that describes the contents of the file but does not actually reveal these contents. This may be useful in a data sharing scenario, where you may be willing or in a position to share your data upon request. A request can only come if someone knows there is something there to request.

When all data and metadata are stored on private media or are unpublished, non-collaborators will have zero knowledge of any of your research data or other files. 

Directory access permissions should be articulated based on collaborator roles during project preparation. In the event that storage systems support file-level permission control, any specific file-permissions should also be considered and articulated. A registry for collaborator permissions should always be maintained, listing all collaborators, their roles, and their access permissions. When leaving a project, collaborators should have their permissions and access updated.

Implementation

Different storage solutions, cloud platforms, operating systems, and applications will have different options and methods for implementing file and folder permissions and controlling ownership of resources. In fact, aspects of permission and ownership may not be within your control on infrastructure provided to you by your institution. 

If you are using a set-up that is supported by your institution, directly contacting the responsible unit(s) and reviewing their support documentation is the best way to learn about how the platforms work. It is not uncommon for institutional implementations to differ from publicly available implementations of the same system.
