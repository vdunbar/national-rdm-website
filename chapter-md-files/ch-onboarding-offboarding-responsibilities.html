Onboarding, Offboarding, and Assigning Responsibilities

Well-designed onboarding and offboarding protocols are fundamental to the success of a research lab or community of practice. This is particularly true in situations where there is frequent turnover of research members and participants, whether this be undergraduate assistants, graduate students, post-doctoral fellows, or faculty.

The intent of this section is not to define how one should engage in onboarding and offboarding generally, but to highlight key issues related to Research Data Management, that when properly handled as part of bringing people on or seeing them off, help to ensure the many tenets of RDM articulated in this text, including such things as reproducibility, integrity, control and ownership, etc.

This section can be read in two ways: first, from the perspective of someone entering a lab as a guide to the kinds of questions one should be asking if they are not explicitly addressed by whomever is welcoming you into the space; and second, from the perspective of someone coordinating a lab as a guide to the kinds of protocols that should be put into place to encourage and enable proper RDM processes.

Assigning Responsibilities

The purpose of assigning responsibilities to members of the lab related to RDM is threefold: 1) to distribute work across a potentially diverse team with different levels of responsibility to the lab or project, 2) to assign accountability, and 3) to provide an answer to the question ‘who should I ask about… ?’

Having a set of well-defined roles and responsibilities related to RDM at the lab or team level and a standard set of roles and responsibilities associated with each new research project will ensure that work is streamlined and adheres to institutional or funder requirements and RDM best practices. As such, roles and responsibilities may need to be designated at the team or lab level as well as at the individual project level.

Things to consider when assigning responsibilities to specific roles or people include:

  • Experience and growth potential of lab members
  • The need to ensure that information is both up-to-date and timely
  • Implications, if relevant, to ethics or agreements with research partners or funders
  • Adhering to institutional, funder, and publisher policies and any other jurisdictional requirements that impact how research data are managed, such as laws governing data sovereignty

Communicating responsibilities

Communicate responsibilities associated with specific roles or people in the most appropriate place. This communication may be a general onboarding or reference document for high level responsibilities or for aspects related to responsibilities that are universal and not project dependent. It may be in a readme file in a specific folder associated with a specific project, because this is where someone would encounter the need for this information.

Ensuring responsibilities are properly implemented can include some system of checks and balances or periodic monitoring. Despite one’s best efforts, errors and mistakes are a normal part of the research process. Having processes in place to monitor roles and responsibilities can help catch and address these errors. This is of particular importance for projects where there is a high level of risk associated with mis-handled data, such as a privacy breach.

Keep in mind that:

  • There is little harm in a reasonable amount of redundancy
  • Not everything is understood the first time around
  • Documentation articulating responsibilities should be up to date with a system to indicate how current documentation is
  • Issues are more easily addressed if communication is early and often

Onboarding

The purpose of standardized onboarding procedures is to make tacit (implied, not stated) practices more explicit to ensure people can work together more easily on shared projects and tasks. These procedures should set up new team members, lab members, or research partners to succeed from the start, directing them to the most appropriate resources. In the context of RDM, this includes knowing who to talk to and where to find resources and establishing the baseline conventions (file naming, file storage, etc.) they should be using.

Getting new members off on the right RDM foot will help save time (issues locating files, interpreting outdated documentation, etc.), limit risk (accidental exposure of sensitive data, recovery of lost files with backups, etc.), and ensure data are being properly purposed (ownership and control, licencing, etc.)

When developing onboarding procedures and documentation, or when being onboarded into a research lab or community of practice, the following are things that should be addressed and that are covered in this text:

  • Storage locations
  • File backup procedures
  • File access and permissions
  • File organization and directory structures
  • Required documentation
  • Conventions for file naming and version control
  • Preferred file formats
  • Preferred licenses

Offboarding

Once a lab member or research partner has moved elsewhere, it can be challenging to solicit information from them related to a project they were previously involved in with you. And the longer your research project operates, the more likely you will have people leave the research team. As it relates to RDM, having a standard process for offboarding people will help prevent or reduce:

  • People taking passwords and/or files with them
  • Time lost locating files required for continued work
  • Possible file security breaches in the future
