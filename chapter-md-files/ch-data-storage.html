Data Storage

Note 

This section is about mediums of storage, locations of these mediums, and utility of various mediums for data processing and preservation. Organizing data files is covered elsewhere; see file naming for how to standardize your file names – see directory structures for how to standardize your directory structures. 

Data need to be stored. A simple statement, but not one without its complexities. Key aspects to data storage include access to the data by the researcher or research team, an appropriate level of security for the data being stored, and redundancy in storage solutions, i.e. having back-ups. 

Discussion

Data storage is a complex issue involving file size determination, privacy and security, storage tied to specific software applications, with more and more of these applications operating in the cloud, jurisdictional requirements, transfer considerations, etc. The following is a relatively brief discussion of a few of these issues. 

Often, your IT department will handle storage solutions used jointly by researchers and administrative teams, forming the backbone of most day-to-day storage options, while your Advanced Research Computing team will handle research-specific storage solutions that cater to things like large data transfer and storage or high-performance computing. Finally, your library will handle data repositories that support long term storage of data or storage to support outputs such as publications. 

Data storage can be grouped into several categories. One is by medium, or the technology, used to store data. Another is by location of these storage devices. And a third is by whether or not a storage location allows one to actively work with data on that storage platform. Each of these facets of data storage will play into how one implements data storage for their research. 

Storage by Activity

Generally, storage is conceptually divided into two activity categories: active storage and long-term storage. 

Active Storage

Ease of access is critical when actively working with data. For this reason, active storage locations are generally close at hand, such as a physical hard disk. This can also include active storage over a network, whether that be an institutional network or the Internet. The further the data need to travel (internal hard drive < external hard drive < local network < internet), the slower one’s access to these data.

Active data storage options also need to account for who is working with the data. One person, with one computer may be well served by an internal hard drive. However, if that person is working off multiple computers, or is working with collaborators within or outside their institution, storage systems that are connected to a network or the Internet are more practical. 

Your primary active data location should balance this need for quick access alongside distributed access. At the same time, increased distribution heightens the risk of data being accessed by unintended audiences. 

Long-term storage 

Long-term storage generally refers to the storage of files after the completion of a project, but can refer to storage of data for later access in larger projects. 

Compared to active storage, ease of access to long-term storage is less important, since the files are no longer in frequent use and are not undergoing frequent changes. However, long-term stability and potentially discoverability by the wider community are of greater concern. For this reason, long-term storage usually makes use of a third-party data repository, which provides robust metadata schemas that ensure that data are searchable and discoverable. Many also offer a variety of embargo and restricted access options, including public release of metadata only. Others offer convenient storage of extremely large data sets. 

Files in long term storage often need to be migrated to a different storage medium and location to be worked on. 

Storage Locations

Storage locations may be local or remote, both of which can be beneficial. It is not uncommon to rely on both mediums as a way to facilitate efficient data workflows and ensure data is backed up. 

Hard Drives 

Hard drives can include both the storage on your personal computer and external storage devices. These are often referred to as local storage, and are often the simplest option for the active storage of smaller to mid-range projects. A key benefit of hard drives is the ease of access to data and files because no Internet connection is required, and the files are directly accessible by your computer. 

It is recommended to encrypt files on local devices, which helps keep your data secure. As long as the devices remain in your possession, your data’s privacy and security will be maintained. With that said, the physical location in which you keep your computer(s) and external device(s) will be a primary factor in upholding the privacy and security of your data, and you want to always be conscious of who can access them. Another privacy consideration with using local storage is when traveling and crossing borders with data, as devices can be subject to being searched. 

A primary limitation of local storage is the difficulty of sharing data with project collaborators. It is common to use local storage in conjunction with remote storage to facilitate data sharing and to back your data up. 

Network Storage 

Network storage is a form of remote storage, usually made available through institutions, and is likely hosted on-campus, but may be part of shared services across multiple institutions and located at a partner campus. 

This type of storage is usually reserved for active data, but your institution may also provide long-term network storage. Strengths of this type of storage are that it will often meet any on-premise requirements that your data may have, and it facilitates easy data sharing with collaborators at your institution. Network storage is often very private and secure, and because it is usually backed-up automatically, it is valuable in safeguarding your data. 

A drawback of network storage is that it can be challenging to share with collaborators outside your institution, and is often limited to use by faculty. Although network storage may be accessible through an Internet connection off campus, the connection and data transfer are usually not as fluid as with cloud storage. 

Cloud Storage 

Cloud storage can be considered the most remote storage, as the data can be hosted on servers worldwide. It is generally the easiest way to share data with collaborators outside your institution and is usually necessary in these types of collaborations. 

The primary considerations with cloud storage revolve around privacy and security restrictions, and it is worth knowing where the data is being stored (domestically or internationally), and what security measures the cloud provider supports. Cloud storage is almost always provided by a third-party vendor (i.e. not actively managed by your institution), and may have costs associated with it (either for your institution or individuals), and therefore may not always be a viable option. 

Back ups

Most data can not be reasonably or easily re-collected within a single study, and losing data can be a challenge to recover from, so it is important to secure multiple copies to safeguard against loss. 

In the section on file naming we discuss strategies for managing multiple copies and iterations of data files in a single storage location. Here, we address the prevention of data loss with up-to-date backups of your files, and how many copies of files and the locations of these copies matters. 

There are many approaches to backing up files. One common approach is the 3-2-1 model:  

  •  3 copies in 

  •  2 formats (i.e. laptop hard drive and removable hard drive) with 

  •  1 stored off-site (i.e. a different physical location to protect from physical damage or disasters) 

You will encounter other models. The important thing is that data are in multiple physical locations and that backups are routine (i.e.,  scheduled back-ups). Whether the schedule is daily, weekly, bi-weekly, etc. depends completely on how safe proof you would like to keep your data. 

While these backups can be maintained manually, automated backup workflows are more convenient and, therefore, more likely to be used regularly - ensuring that backed-up versions of files are always current. 

Note 

Cloud storage can provide automatic backups and local syncing, but it is not always the perfect solution we might hope it would be. 

Historically, syncing often meant duplicating content in the cloud with content on the synced device, but this is increasingly not the case. Instead, a ‘just in time’ approach is used, where only files actively being used are synced to your local machine. This means that where once using cloud storage meant you had a backup system in place, this may no longer be the case. 

Syncing can also interfere with data processing. If you use an application that does real time processing on your data (image editing, audio editing, scripting, etc), these processes can conflict with automatic syncing processes, which inadvertently update files between local storage and the cloud. 

Estimating Storage Space

Accurately estimating storage space when you start your project is crucial. You will need to allocate storage for original data, processed and analyzed data, documentation that informs users about your data, draft papers, charts, visualizations, photos, and supplementary items that go within your papers. You will also need space for the administrative documents of your project, such as grant applications. Consult your with your institutional research support units if you are uncertain about how to estimate your storage requirements. Arrange for sufficient or additional storage well before it is actually required. Sometimes you have instant access to needed additional storage; in other cases, it takes time to gain access. 

Data Privacy and Security

In file permissions and ownership we discuss implementing access controls to data files, which is a key aspect of privacy. In this section we talk more about security. Privacy relates to who can and should have access to data and the risks associated with the release of data beyond these individuals. Security refers to the technology used to limit data exposure beyond those designated with access. Data may have privacy concerns but not be secure, just as data may be secure but have no privacy concerns. One’s institution generally governs the security of the data storage infrastructure. A process known as a Privacy Impact Assessment is how most institutions determine the suitability of a specific piece of software or storage solution for specific data related activities. 

Privacy Impact Assessments (PIA) 

A privacy impact assessment is a risk management and review process that mitigates threats to data security and unnecessary privacy violations. Depending on your jurisdiction or local policy, they may be required when using a new service or piece of software for a project. They may also be suggested or required during data access requests, for example when requesting access to highy sensitive data from a health authority or other government body.  

Public and External Access Considerations 

The nature of external or public access to a project is also significant to a project’s data management. It is important to have an awareness of the information privacy laws of the jurisdictions in which you work. Data access and permissions are especially important when sensitive data are involved, and ensuring adequate privacy is maintained through appropriate security measures should be a fundamental objective of the project from its inception. In terms of public and external access, consider: 

  • Data access requirements should reflect institutional policies and mandates 

  • Access standards may be affected by grant-specific requirements 

  • Providing information about institutional data access policies and mechanisms for external interest 

  • Any ethical obligations or desires of research participants and collaborators.
