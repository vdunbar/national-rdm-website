Data Deposit

What is Data Deposition

Data deposition is the act of putting your data in a repository that is, generally, accessible by people outside of your research team. Usually, this is done when a research project, or a noteworthy portion of a research project, has wrapped up. This involves moving a copy of your data from your active project storage locations, which you can read more about in data storage, to a storage platform dedicated to preserving data for long-term access and discovery. 

What is a Data Repository

A data repository is a digital platform that hosts data and research materials alongside relevant metadata. It allows you to store, manage, publish, and enable the discovery of your work. Many data repositories support long-term preservation of files, and possibly some level of curation to ensure that data and associated files meet a certain standard of quality. 

Why Would I Deposit My Data

There are many reasons why you may end up moving your data into a repository. While the points below are not exhaustive, they provide a strong overview of the various motivations to deposit data. 

Research is about more than reporting on findings 

Academic research has historically been measured on final outputs such as publications. This landscape is changing; increasingly funders, distributors, producers, and consumers of research are recognizing that what goes into informing a final output is of equal value to many of the pieces that go into generating those outputs, including not just data, but other ancillary products such as data collection tools, data cleaning processes etc. In a trusted repository, deposits are one way to disseminate and receive recognition for these generated products when they have been constructed in house and to ensure credit is given where it is due when they have been re-used. 

Transparency and Reproducibility 

The only way to verify the reported outcomes of any study is to be able to see everything that went into producing those reported outcomes. This would include access to things like data collections tools, the collected data, and the tools or methods used to analyze these data. The degree to which this is feasible, or even desirable, will be largely disciplinary and project dependent. But a great way of enhancing the transparency and reproducibility of your work is by depositing your data. Read more about transparency and reproducibility in Why is research data management important? 

Accelerating Research 

It’s not uncommon for researchers to be working through similar research questions, or focusing on topics that have significant overlap. However, if data for these projects aren’t shared, every project must essentially start from scratch in regards to data, which is a drain on both time and financial resources. By opening up your data and sharing it with a broader audience, you are supporting future research efforts, and speeding up the rate at which certain research questions can be answered. It is not uncommon for researchers to work exclusively with previously collected data or to seek out similar data to what they’re collecting to help validate their own data. 

Funder & Publisher Mandates 

A frequently encountered reason to deposit your data is because you simply have to. Funders and publishers are increasingly requiring involved projects to make their data as available as possible. This is often driven by the same desires of transparency and reproducibility that represent the foundation of good research. Whenever your project secures funder support or is considering a relationship with publishers, it is vital to evaluate their data deposition policies and make sure that you can satisfy them. Data repositories can function both as a way to satisfy requirements for data deposits, but also as a way to satisfy data retention requirements. A benefit of several data repositories is that they offer long-term preservation, as well as assigning a digital object identifier (DOI) for the data, as a way to provide persistent access. 

What Should I Deposit?

This is a difficult question to address. We have discussed a number of different iterations of data in this text, including original data, which tends to be in a state not suited to analysis, ‘cleaned data’, which would form the basis of analysis, ‘subsets of data’ which might be used in specific applications, data you didn’t collect, data you don’t own etc. 

The most appropriate deposit will consequently factor in many questions, starting with, is it yours to deposit? If you’re unsure about the answer to this question, it is recommended you speak with your Library to determine whether you are in a position to do so. Assuming that you are in a position to deposit data, there are some general guidelines below to help you consider what you might deposit. One of the main factors to guide this decision should be based around identifying why you are depositing the data, and what you are hoping others can gain from it. 

Original Data 

This term refers to the data you have collected in its uncleaned, or unprocessed, form. This can be valuable to deposit if promoting the transparency and validity of your results is of importance, as such a deposit allows for a more fulsome story of the journey of your data from collection to preparation for analysis, to interpretation, helping to limit or elucidate bias. 

Cleaned Data 

This is data that has undergone some form of processing, formatting, or general “cleaning”, to help give the data shape and structure to allow for analysis and potential subsetting, whether this be the final set of categorical tags used in coding transcripts or the results of normalizing a tabular data set. This can be valuable to deposit if allowing others to query the data that you have gathered and contributing to academic discourse is of importance. These can also help build larger datasets for further inquiry; consider for example deposit in platforms like GenBank or GBIF which are global deposit sites for cleaned, disciplinary connected data. Additionally, this can be a way to share de-identified or otherwise treated sensitive data that shouldn’t otherwise be made public. 

Data Subsets 

This is data that could either be original or cleaned, but only represents a portion of the data that you have collected. Depositing this can be valuable for the same reasons as depositing the full version of either original or cleaned data, but noting that you may still be working through other sections of the data and you do not necessarily want your work to get “scooped”. This can also include removing sensitivities from your data that shouldn’t be made public. Data deposited to support publication is frequently a subset of all data collected. 

Supporting Materials & Documentation 

A key aspect in ensuring that your data is interpretable to those outside of your research group is including proper documentation to support your data (for more info, see Documentation). In addition to including the necessary documentation and metadata, it is also very helpful to include any ancillary products, such as dictionaries, scripts, or details regarding software that were used to help generate your results. 

File Formats 

Depositing files in popular non-proprietary formats will ensure that the widest possible audience can access and open them, hopefully for years to come. While this isn’t always possible, we encourage you to consult the File Formats section for information and recommendations to help facilitate this. 

Where Should I Deposit My Data?

There is an increasingly large number of data repositories, and finding the right one can be challenging. This section will attempt to address the kinds of repositories that exist and some of the limitations of a variety of repositories, with the goal of providing you with the key questions to ask of your institution’s research support team and peers when you’re looking for an appropriate place to deposit your data. 

Repository Categories 

Data repositories can be grouped in several ways. 

One is by intended purpose:  

  • to support publication and funder requirements 

  • to support further research on similar data collected by many research teams 

Another is by scope of accepted content:  

  • some, generalist repositories, will accept anything 

  • others, disciplinary repositories, will only accept data related to specific areas of research 

  • others, research domain specific repositories, will only accept data related to specific kinds of inquiry, like sequences from genetic sequencing. 

Another is by owner, funder, or administrative structure:  

  • some are institution specific - many post-secondary institutions have their own hosting repository 

  • some are government funded and coordinated 

  • some are owned and managed by societies or other non-governmental agencies 

  • some are owned and managed by corporate entities involved in some aspect of the research life cycle, perhaps most notably in the realm of publication. 

These categories are not mutually exclusive, but can help you ask the right questions when looking for a place to deposit. For example, indicating why you are depositing, whether there is value in your data living alongside other disciplinary related data, and how close you want your data to your research institution, a publisher, or disciplinary related society. When there is this much choice, there is no right or wrong answer, but perhaps sometimes a better or less than ideal answer. 

Repository Evaluation 

In addition to selecting an appropriate ‘type’ of repository, repositories will, like any piece of software, offer various features, some limiting, some essential for your kind of research data. Consequently, important general considerations to keep top of mind when looking at a repository include the following: 

  • Maximum file size supported 

  • Maximum dataset size allowed 

  • Cost 

  • Data licensing requirements 

  • Data preservation considerations 

  • User accessibility 

  • Data privacy and security 

  • Repository reputation 

  • Infrastructure and planning for the repository’s continued existence 

  • Metadata accessibility 

  • Data use and citation metrics  

  • Curation of datasets to maintain high quality standards and general organization 

  • Data provenance recording 

  • Data interaction capabilities 

While these criteria are important, you may not have a lot of control over them depending on what repositories are available for your specific needs. 

Finding a Repository

Once you know what you need in a repository, the next step is to find an appropriate repository that meets these needs! A quick search on your favourite search engine will demonstrate the plethora of repositories available, which in turn can be frustrating to navigate. 

Generally, your institution will have a number of generalist repositories that they recommend and for which you can easily get answers to all of the considerations posed above. And generally, these repositories are under the purview of the library. Your library will also likely have familiarity with certain disciplinary repositories and can help guide you through these. When it comes to highly specialized repositories, often a bit more investigation is needed, and consultation with peers in your discipline. 

If you are interested in exploring more broadly but with some guidance, there are also many curated lists of repositories; recommended lists are available in the resources section of this chapter. 

Sensitive Data

Generally speaking, repositories are not suitable for sensitive data. This is because most of the repositories that you will come across are hosted in the cloud and connected to the Internet, creating an inherent security risk. But there are caveats to this. Some repositories store your data and meta data independently of each other, allowing your research data to be securely protected, but your metadata readily accessible. This allows for discoverability of your data, but not direct access; access can be handled on a request by request basis. 

Many repositories are increasingly exploring the role of encryption in the deposit of sensitive data; this is an ongoing area of inquiry. 

If your data are sensitive, but would benefit from discoverability through metadata alongside potential restricted access upon request, this should ideally be investigated on a case by case basis. Your institution’s library or research computing units should be your first point of contact.
